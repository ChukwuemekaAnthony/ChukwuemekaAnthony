
# Commercial Bid Intelligence Platform
## Economic Framework

### Executive Overview
In service organizations, large contracts are won or lost during the bidding stage.  
Yet many organizations evaluate bids using fragmented spreadsheets, individual judgment, and inconsistent financial assumptions.

The Commercial Bid Intelligence Platform introduces a structured economic framework that allows organizations to evaluate every bid opportunity through the same financial lens.

The objective is simple:

**Ensure every bid pursued creates measurable economic value.**

---

## The Commercial Problem
Commercial teams face a recurring dilemma:

1. Some bids generate large revenue but very low margins.
2. Some bids require significant pursuit effort but have low probability of winning.
3. Some bids appear profitable until risk factors are considered.
4. Some bids create cash exposure before revenue begins.
5. Portfolio capacity limits how many bids can be pursued simultaneously.

Without a consistent economic model, organizations often pursue bids that ultimately destroy value.

---

## Core Economic Drivers of Service Contracts

### 1. Revenue Potential
The expected annual revenue generated by delivering the service.

### 2. Delivery Cost Structure
The cost required to deliver the service including labor, equipment, consumables, subcontractors, and overhead.

### 3. Margin Requirement
The profit threshold required to justify the operational risk of the contract.

### 4. Probability of Winning
The realistic likelihood of securing the contract.

### 5. Capital Exposure
The financial investment required before revenue begins.

These drivers determine the true economic value of a bid.

---

## Bid Pursuit Economics
Pursuing a contract is itself an investment.

Bid teams spend time performing:

- technical assessments
- site visits
- commercial modeling
- proposal development
- client engagement

These activities represent a **pursuit cost**.

Organizations must evaluate whether the expected value of a bid justifies the cost required to pursue it.

---

## Contract Profitability Perspective
Revenue alone is not a reliable indicator of success.

High-revenue contracts can still produce weak profitability if:

- operational costs escalate
- risk premiums are underestimated
- commodity markets fluctuate
- mobilization costs increase

A disciplined commercial framework therefore evaluates **profitability relative to revenue**.

---

## Risk-Adjusted Commercial Thinking
Not all contracts carry the same level of risk.

Key factors influencing contract risk include:

- operational complexity
- clarity of the scope of work
- client relationship maturity
- geographic operating conditions

Each factor increases the margin required for the contract to remain economically viable.

---

## Portfolio-Level Economics
Commercial teams rarely pursue a single bid.

Instead, they manage a **portfolio of bid opportunities**.

Portfolio management introduces additional considerations:

- bid team capacity
- total expected revenue pipeline
- probability-weighted contract value
- prioritization of opportunities

---

## Capital Exposure & Mobilization Economics
Many service contracts require capital investment before operations begin.

Examples include:

- recruiting and training personnel
- purchasing or commissioning equipment
- establishing operational infrastructure

This period creates **cash exposure**, where costs are incurred before the first client payment is received.

---

## Strategic Commercial Impact
Organizations implementing structured bid economics experience several improvements:

• more disciplined bid selection  
• improved contract profitability  
• better allocation of commercial resources  
• stronger visibility into revenue pipeline  
• reduced exposure to unprofitable contracts  

The result is a commercial organization that pursues growth **with financial discipline**.

---

## Conclusion
Winning contracts is important.

Winning **profitable** contracts is essential.

By structuring the economics behind bid decisions, organizations ensure commercial growth translates into long‑term financial performance.
